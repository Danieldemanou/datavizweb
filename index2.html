Introduction

This work aims at showing the explosion of refugees from 1999 to 2016 which increased by 62% with a specific focus on the Syrian refugees crisis. The Syrian refugees crisis is by far the biggest crisis the world has ever experienced in the last decade with more than 5 millions Syrian refugees since the beginning of the crisis .
You will also see a map that helps you visualize the movement of the refugees from 1999 to 2016 and the asylum refugees crises associated. Then, we will focus on the Syrian crisis to make you realize the humanitarian disaster behind.

THE HUMANS BEHIND

Children. Women. Men. Those are the people that have escaped from the Syrian war horror.
Each of them has a story, a name, a life, a love story, an age, and everything that makes a LIFE. 69% of those refugees are Men, 18% are Children and 13% are Women.


Refugees struggle with language barriers, finding work,
and meeting people in their temporary new homes.

Because of languages barriers, many struggle to find employment outside of the refugee community itself. As expected, children develop language skills quickly. Yet with adults the barriers of language easily become barriers of understanding. Therefore language is the primary obstacle which divides the refugee from the rest of its citizens. Given the divide which language and politics can pose, images and stories have become the solution to bridge the gap between people. Photos, such as that of Omran Daqneesh, the Syrian boy who became the face of the refugee crisis, remind us that refugees are people not statistics.

Interview with Yazan

Name : Yazan

Age : 35

Nationality : Syrian

Profession : Poet and journalist

Refugee since 2011

While still in Syria, Yazan was called for obligatory military service but left for Jordan, unwilling to work for the Assad regime
Yazan then found himself in the Zaatari refugee camp across the Jordanian border. After one day in the camp, Yazan left due to what he describes as terrible living conditions and a lack of communication with the outside world. Because he was requested for military service, Yazan was unable to go home, and he began living close to the Jordanian border for several months.

Yazan came to France in 2014. Again, he was lucky. Yazanâ€™s story also highlights the difference which connections and assets can make in refugee status. Like others who made the perilous journey to Europe via sea and who lost many loved ones along the way. He did not choose France; however, he is happy and grateful to be there. The main problem, he says, is not being able to find a job. He writes articles where he can, but it only brings in around one hundred euros per month. The language barrier remains a problem. While he is actively seeking work, he is entirely starting from scratch in France. In Syria, he was a journalist. He was also studying law in Damascus. He wants to finish his studies, but there are obstacles. For one, in his mid-thirties, he is too old for the French bursary system, which provides educational funds to refugees. In the future, he would like to study politics
